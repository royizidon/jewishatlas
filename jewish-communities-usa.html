How Jewish America was built

Migration shaped every community on the map

Jewish life in America is not a single story. It is a series of arrivals, each leaving its own geographic footprint.

The earliest communities were Sephardi merchants who settled in port cities — Newport, Charleston, Savannah, New York — in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. Their synagogues, some still standing, are among the oldest Jewish buildings in the Western hemisphere.

The nineteenth century brought Central European immigrants, many of whom moved inland along trade routes, establishing congregations in Cincinnati, St. Louis, Chicago and dozens of smaller Midwestern towns. Their Reform movement built grand temple buildings that remain landmarks in cities where Jewish life has long since contracted.

The mass Eastern European immigration of 1880 to 1924 transformed American Jewish geography. Millions settled in dense urban neighbourhoods — the Lower East Side, Chicago's Maxwell Street, Baltimore's East Baltimore Street. The synagogues, landsmanshaftn and cemeteries they built in those decades account for a large share of what the map records today.

The postwar decades brought suburbanization — the great dispersal from city centres into Long Island, New Jersey, the North Shore of Chicago, the San Fernando Valley. And from the 1960s onward, a sustained movement toward Florida, California and other Sun Belt states created the second tier of large American Jewish communities visible in the data.

Each wave of immigration left its own layer on the map — synagogues, cemeteries, schools — some still active, many now only memory.

Major centres of Jewish life

Eight cities, eight different stories

The largest Jewish communities in America are not simply bigger versions of each other. Each reflects a distinct pattern of settlement, migration and institutional life.

New York metro · most mapped sites in the database

New York

One of the largest Jewish urban concentrations in the world. The five boroughs and the wider metro — Long Island, Teaneck, Passaic, Monsey, Spring Valley — together account for roughly 31 percent of all US sites in the Jewish Atlas database. Borough Park, Williamsburg and Crown Heights each function as distinct Orthodox communities with their own dense institutional infrastructure.

South Florida · second by mapped sites

South Florida

Built almost entirely in the postwar decades, South Florida's Jewish community is notable for its density of kosher establishments — 321 of its mapped sites are kosher restaurants or stores, more than any other American metro in the current database. Miami Beach, Hollywood and Boca Raton each have distinct characters shaped by different waves of arrival.

Los Angeles · largest West Coast community

Los Angeles

Among the largest Jewish communities west of the Mississippi, centred on the Pico-Robertson and Fairfax neighbourhoods but dispersed across a vast metropolitan area. The Valley, the Westside and the beach cities each have their own communities and institutions.

Chicago · historic Midwest centre

Chicago

One of the great historic centres of American Jewish life. Skokie, Rogers Park, and the North Shore suburbs carry the legacy of a community shaped by Eastern European immigration and the labour movement. The Maxwell Street neighbourhood where it once concentrated has been largely demolished.

Philadelphia · one of the oldest communities

Philadelphia

Home to one of the oldest continuously active Jewish communities in North America, with a notable concentration of historic cemeteries — 18 in the metro area — reflecting centuries of settlement. The Main Line suburbs and Northeast Philadelphia were the postwar centres of gravity.

Boston · university presence

Boston

A community shaped strongly by its university presence — Boston has 24 Hillel centers in the database, a high ratio reflecting its density of higher education institutions. Brookline remains the residential and institutional heart; Newton and the North Shore are significant suburban centres.

Baltimore · highest cemetery ratio in database

Baltimore

An old community with a notably high concentration of Jewish cemeteries — 25 in the metro area, the highest ratio relative to total sites among major cities in the current database. The reasons trace back to the independent structure of nineteenth and early twentieth century immigrant congregations, each of which established its own burial ground.

Washington · growing contemporary community

Washington

A growing contemporary community, with 83 synagogues but a striking gap in kosher establishments in the current database — likely a coverage issue rather than a reflection of the actual community. The Maryland suburbs of Bethesda, Rockville and Silver Spring hold much of the residential population.

What the map reveals

Three geographic patterns

The data does not simply confirm what is already known about American Jewish geography. It surfaces contrasts that are harder to see without coordinates.

The New York concentration is not just large — it is categorically different from any other metro. Sites within the greater New York area account for roughly 31 percent of all US sites in the database, more than the next seven metros combined.

In the database as currently mapped, South Florida outranks Los Angeles in total sites (560 vs 403), driven by a high density of kosher establishments — 321 of South Florida's sites are kosher restaurants or stores, compared with 75 in Los Angeles. This likely reflects both a genuinely denser kosher infrastructure and stronger database coverage in that area.

The average geographic centre of US cemeteries sits further north than that of synagogues, which may reflect older settlement patterns that preceded the Sun Belt migration — though the database's cemetery coverage is less complete than its synagogue coverage, and this pattern should be treated as preliminary.

Metro totals are counted by coordinate bounding box, not by city name. The same site may be named differently by different contributors; the figures are working aggregations, not authoritative counts.

A closer look

Beyond the major cities

The most historically layered parts of the map are not always the largest. Cemeteries, former synagogues and the records of dissolved congregations carry the geography of an older America.

Across the South and Midwest, Jewish communities established in the nineteenth century left institutional traces that often outlast the communities themselves. A synagogue building repurposed as a church, a cemetery maintained by a society with no living members, a Hillel center at a university where Jewish students now number in the dozens — these are the map's historic layer.

Baltimore · a case study in Jewish institutional history

Why one city has 25 Jewish cemeteries

Baltimore has a notably high number of Jewish cemeteries relative to its total mapped sites — 25 cemeteries among 119 sites in the database, a ratio higher than any other major city in the current dataset. The historical reasons for this pattern are documented in Baltimore's Jewish communal records, including the Jewish Cemetery Association of Baltimore's published surveys and the history of the city's immigrant congregations.

Jewish law requires that Jews be buried among other Jews in consecrated ground. As waves of Eastern European immigrants arrived in Baltimore from the 1880s onward, they did not build a single unified community — they built dozens of independent congregations, landsmanshaftn (hometown societies), and mutual aid organizations, each of which secured its own burial plot as one of its first acts. The Workmen's Circle, the Hebrew Young Men's Sick Relief Association, and scores of individual synagogues each claimed their own ground.

At the time, health regulations pushed cemeteries to the city's outer edges. As Baltimore expanded through the twentieth century, those once-rural plots were absorbed into residential neighbourhoods — leaving behind a scattered landscape of small, independent burial grounds that no consolidation ever unified.

Today the Jewish Cemetery Association of Baltimore oversees many of these sites. Their continued existence, mapped as 25 separate points in the Jewish Atlas database, records not just deaths but the particular way Jewish immigrant life organised itself in one American city.

The interior

Jewish institutions exist in every part of the country — including cities far from the major concentrations of the Northeast, South Florida and the coasts.

Congregation Beth Aaron in Billings, Montana. Jackson Hole Jewish Community Center in Wyoming. Temple Mizpah in Abilene, Texas. Temple B'nai Israel in Amarillo. These are not the remnants of larger communities — many are active institutions serving small but continuous Jewish populations in cities that never made it onto the standard map of American Jewish life.

The database records their existence. How distant they are from neighbouring communities, and what that distance means for the people who sustain them, is a question the map invites but cannot answer alone.

Frequently asked questions

On Jewish communities in America

Where are the largest Jewish communities in the United States?

By number of mapped sites in the Jewish Atlas database, the New York metro area is by far the largest, with sites across the five boroughs, Long Island and the New Jersey suburbs accounting for roughly 31 percent of all US entries. South Florida, Los Angeles, Chicago, Philadelphia and Boston follow. Within New York, Brooklyn — particularly Borough Park, Williamsburg, Midwood and Crown Heights — holds an extraordinary concentration of Jewish institutions.

Which cities have the most Jewish institutions?

New York City has 917 entries under the administrative city name alone in the database, with thousands more under borough and neighbourhood names. Lakewood, New Jersey — a city of around 120,000 — has 302 mapped sites, making it one of the most institutionally dense Jewish communities in the world relative to its size. Los Angeles, Chicago, Miami and Philadelphia follow among the largest centres outside New York.

Are there historic Jewish communities in the American South?

Yes. Some of the oldest Jewish communities in America were established in Southern port cities — Savannah, Charleston and New Orleans had significant Sephardi communities by the eighteenth century. Across the South and Midwest, Jewish merchants and tradespeople established congregations in small cities during the nineteenth century. Many of these communities have contracted or disappeared, but their cemeteries, former synagogue buildings and institutional records often remain — and appear in the Jewish Atlas database as the historic layer of the map.

Does the map include former synagogues and cemeteries?

Yes. The Jewish Atlas database includes active, historic and former sites. A building that functioned as a synagogue and was later converted to another use, or a cemetery established by a congregation that no longer exists, both appear on the map. This is what distinguishes the map from a directory of current Jewish life — it preserves the geographic record of communities that existed, not only those that exist today.

Does Jewish Atlas include every Jewish site in the United States?

No. The database is an ongoing project and coverage varies by region and site type. Active synagogues in large metropolitan areas are well represented; smaller communities, historic sites in rural areas, and certain categories of institution are less complete. The figures on this page reflect the current state of the database and will change as more sites are documented. If you know of a site that should be included, you can add it directly through the map at jewishatlas.org.
