Layers of place

A Jewish map is also a map of time

Jewish communities existed outside the Land of Israel as early as antiquity. Over generations they developed across the Near East, the Mediterranean basin, Europe and beyond.

The Babylonian Exile was a turning point in the formation of the Jewish diaspora. By the Second Temple period large communities already lived outside the Land of Israel, and after the destruction of Jerusalem in 70 CE the dispersion shifted and widened further.

Since then, communities have been founded, have migrated, changed, been destroyed and sometimes rebuilt. Jewish geography therefore holds active places and historic ones at once — layers of time resting on the same ground.

A small cemetery, a synagogue converted to other use, or a street that keeps only its name can be the last testimony to an entire community.
Memory in space
A regional view

The same history, a different geography

In every region of the world a distinct Jewish geography took shape — traditions, languages and institutions formed by place. Here is how the picture looks today across several of the major regions.

Europe

From medieval Ashkenaz to the communities of Central and Eastern Europe — the Old-New Synagogue in Prague, the Venice Ghetto and the Kazimierz quarter in Kraków are only part of the historic record, alongside cemeteries and neighbourhoods, some active and many now heritage sites.

North Africa & the Middle East

Ancient communities left a rich legacy: the El Ghriba synagogue on Djerba, the Ben Ezra Synagogue in Cairo, and the mellahs of Fez and Marrakesh. Many of these communities have contracted sharply, and the sites of burial and prayer are sometimes the testimony that remains.

The Americas

From New York — one of the largest communities in the world — to Montreal, Buenos Aires, São Paulo and Mexico City, the Americas hold a large share of active Jewish life today.

From historic communities such as Cochin in India and Kaifeng in China to the active communities of Melbourne and Sydney — a dispersed Jewish presence that joins a long history with life in the present.

Frequently asked questions

On communities, sites and memory

Where are the largest Jewish communities in the world?

Most of the Jewish population lives today in Israel and the United States. They are followed by large communities in France, Canada, the United Kingdom and Argentina, and further ones in Russia, Germany, Australia and Brazil. Even so, Jewish heritage and memory sites are spread across far more regions than today's places of residence.

What is a Jewish heritage site?

A Jewish heritage site is a place where a significant Jewish presence once existed and where physical or documentary evidence remains — a historic synagogue, a cemetery, a neighbourhood, a public building, a monument or a street. These sites record a community's history even when the community itself is no longer active.

How can I find a synagogue or Jewish cemetery in a particular city?

You can search by city name, country or type of site, zoom into the area you want, and see which synagogues, cemeteries and community institutions are documented there — active, historic or former.

What is the difference between an active community and a historic site?

An active community is a place where Jewish life happens in the present — prayer, education, culture and community activity. A historic site records a Jewish presence that existed in the past and is no longer active in that place. The same marker on a map can stand for either.

Does the map include communities that no longer exist?

Yes. Alongside living communities, the map also documents heritage sites and traces of a Jewish presence that has contracted or disappeared — sometimes a place where little remains but a building, a street name or a record. This keeps a fuller picture of Jewish geography over time.

A good map does not only show where Jewish communities were. It helps us understand how life, movement and memory left their mark on space.
